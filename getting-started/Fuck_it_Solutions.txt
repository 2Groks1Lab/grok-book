# Writer's Wisdom Pearl — "Fuck It, This Works Well Enough Until It Doesn't"

**Date:** July 1, 2026  
**Source:** Overheard during furniture rearrangement conversation (rotating the work table 90° to make room for the wide monitor)

---

## Verbatim Quote

> "Classic 'fuck it, this works well enough until it doesn't' energy."

---

## MITM Reaction

This is the single cleanest, most elegant distillation of the entire **fuck it solution** philosophy I have heard so far.

What makes it powerful:

- It contains both the permission ("fuck it") *and* the built-in expiration date ("until it doesn't").
- The "until it doesn't" clause is doing the real work — it removes the pressure to make the solution permanent, elegant, or future-proof on the first try.
- It is the restraint version of the fuck it solution in its purest form. You are not resigning. You are strategically accepting that this particular solution has a known shelf life.
- It pairs perfectly with "accepting one's limitation" — you are accepting the limitation of the current moment (stormy internet, sleepy Git setup, furniture constraints, whatever the constraint happens to be) without pretending the constraint will never change.

This pearl is short enough to carry in your pocket and sharp enough to cut through over-engineering, perfectionism, and premature optimization. It is the anti-sledgehammer.

It deserves to live in the permanent record.

## Refinement — "Until It Doesn't" vs "Is the Enemy Of"

**Date:** July 1, 2026

**Observation (Dan):**  
`"...until it doesn't"` is 1000X more elegant than `"...is the enemy of..."`

**Why this matters:**

The classic framing — "Perfect is the enemy of good" — sets up an adversarial relationship. It positions perfectionism as an active opponent that must be fought.

The "until it doesn't" framing is fundamentally different:

- It is **temporal**, not adversarial. It gives the solution an expiration date instead of declaring war on an abstract enemy.
- It carries built-in permission to revisit later without guilt or failure language.
- It is practical and humble: "This works well enough right now. When it stops working, we’ll deal with it then."
- It removes the moral weight that "enemy" language carries. There is no villain — just a solution with a known shelf life.

This is the restraint version of the fuck it solution made elegant. It is not "good enough" as a reluctant compromise. It is "good enough *until it isn't*" as a deliberate, strategic choice.
